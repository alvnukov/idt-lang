## 28. Publication Readiness Gate

The goal is not publication text.

The goal is readiness to enter publication mode without overstating the theory.

### 28.1. Publication modes

There are four distinct publication modes.

Mode A — internal research notes:

the theory may contain unresolved notation, provisional labels, and incomplete gates.

Mode B — public research programme:

the theory may be published if all claims are bounded, gaps are explicit, and no bridge is presented as a completed derivation unless it is actually closed.

Mode C — predictive framework:

the theory may claim independent physical value only after at least one non-refitted bridge produces a number or residual checked against an independent gate.

Mode D — completed physical theory:

the theory may claim replacement/completion of QM/GR only after the core equations, constants, and domains are derived and tested.

Current target:

$$
\text{Mode B readiness}
$$

not:

$$
\text{Mode C or D}.
$$

### 28.2. Claim boundary

Allowed public claim:

> This is a reconstruction programme for a protolanguage in which quantum and relativistic readouts are derived or constrained from inherited distinguishability, clock-rate ratios, and experimental gates.

Forbidden public claims at the current stage:

1. "QM has been derived from first principles";
2. "GR has been derived from first principles";
3. "\(G_N\), \(\hbar\), or \(\alpha_{\mathrm{em}}\) has been numerically predicted";
4. "dark matter/dark energy are explained";
5. "Einstein equations are derived";
6. "the theory is experimentally confirmed".

Required public qualifier:

> The current result is a logically controlled reconstruction framework with conditional weak-field bridges and explicit no-refit gates.

### 28.3. Public value proposition

The public value of the programme is methodological and test-directed before it
is predictive.

The theory is worth developing because it aims to provide:

1. a single reconstruction language separating primitives, readouts,
   calibrations, and blocked assumptions;
2. an executable gate ledger that makes overclaims, circular fits, and
   post-fit residuals visible;
3. a route to dimensionless no-refit tests before any claim about derived
   dimensional constants;
4. calibrated branches that use one declared anchor and then expose the result
   to holdout checks;
5. a reusable verifier for excluding weak bridges quickly rather than
   accumulating informal algebra;
6. a disciplined interface between foundational theory, empirical datasets,
   and publishable negative results;
7. a claim-ledger pattern that can be reused by AI research agents to separate
   evidence, calibration, hypothesis, and unresolved assumptions.

If the programme succeeds, it could give a controlled way to test whether
quantum, weak-gravity, and residual readouts share a deeper inherited
distinguishability structure. If it fails, the verifier and gate ledger should
still produce useful exclusions of candidate bridges and post-fit explanations.

### 28.4. Human and AI research value

The theory does not claim to explain artificial intelligence.

Core AI-facing claim:

> IDT is a candidate executable language for interpreting the physical structure
> of the universe, designed for both human researchers and AI scientific agents.
> Its goal is not to replace physics by assertion, but to provide a disciplined
> map from observations to readouts, from hypotheses to bounded bridges, and
> from assumptions to explicit anchors and gates. If matured, IDT could become
> a shared world-interpretation layer: a way for humans and AI systems to
> organize evidence, test explanations, expose gaps, and progressively deepen
> structural understanding of the physical world.

This is the central AI value: not an explanation of AI, but a possible
human-and-machine language for organizing and extending knowledge of the
physical world.

An AI system using this language would not merely generate plausible
theoretical text. It would be able to:

1. translate observations into declared readouts rather than unstructured
   descriptions;
2. express hypotheses as bounded bridges between primitives and physical
   domains;
3. state whether each bridge is derived, calibrated, conditional, open, or
   blocked;
4. freeze assumptions before checking data;
5. submit candidate explanations to executable gates;
6. keep failed, near-miss, and surviving world-model candidates comparable
   under one manifest.

This makes IDT useful as a possible world-interpretation language, benchmark,
and control layer for scientific reasoning agents. The positive target is an
AI-usable map of the difference between evidence, readout, calibration,
prediction, and unresolved structure.

### 28.5. Minimum readiness checklist

The theory is ready to enter publication mode B only if:

1. canonical source is versioned by git commit and annotated tag;
2. primitive/readout distinction is explicit;
3. actualization calculus has domain conditions and theorem block;
4. \(\hbar_I\), \(G_I\), and \(\alpha_{\mathrm{em},I}\) are not presented as numerically derived;
5. clock-rate route separates derived redshift from conditional Poisson candidate;
6. volume and source-density circularity is removed;

9. spatial-curvature gap \(\gamma_I^{\mathrm{PPN}}\to1\) is explicit;
10. experimental gate ledger exists;
11. unresolved items are listed before any public claim;
12. no residual is introduced after fitting a dataset.

### 28.7. Publication transition rule

Before writing or circulating a public manuscript, create a short public-facing claim sheet with:

1. one-sentence thesis;
2. what is derived;
3. what is conditional;
4. what is calibrated;
5. what is explicitly not derived;
6. which objects are structural selectors, dimensional anchors,
   dimensionless couplings, bridge assumptions, derived readouts,
   experimental gates, or blocked claims;
7. why the framework is useful even before completion;
8. first independent no-refit target;
9. experimental gate ledger status.

If a claim cannot be placed in one of these categories, it must not be published.
