# Origin and Motivation

IDT began from a long-running curiosity about science: physics, chemistry, and
the question of why the world has the structure it has. In recent years that
curiosity became focused on quantum mechanics. The formal tools can be learned
and used, but a deeper question kept returning: what kind of underlying
language would make the behavior of physical systems intelligible rather than
only calculable?

The first move was not to propose a new physical law. It was to isolate a more
primitive unknown: what must be distinguishable, inherited, actualized,
measured, or blocked for a physical claim to become meaningful? IDT grew from
that question into a candidate protolanguage for describing the properties of
this unknown and testing how much physical structure can be reconstructed
without hiding assumptions.

That is why the project is organized as a canonical source plus an executable
verifier. The aim is not to let AI systems generate more plausible scientific
prose. The aim is to use AI-assisted engineering and review to expose
assumptions, organize evidence, find contradictions, and turn speculative
bridges into explicit gates.

The long-term ambition is a shared interpretation layer: a language that humans
and AI research agents can both use to reason about physical structure with
clearer boundaries between what is derived, assumed, blocked, or empirically
anchored.

The project is intentionally conservative in its public claims. Its present
value is not that it has completed a new theory of physics. Its value is that it
tries to make the path toward such a theory inspectable: every strong claim
should either be grounded, tested, bounded, or marked as open.
